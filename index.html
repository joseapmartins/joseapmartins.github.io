About

I've graduated in Physics (five years course) at the University of Lisbon. I started my scientific career in a State Laboratory, working with a Van de Graaff generator doing Rutherford backscattering experiments. I entered the polymer field after obtaining a degree in Materials Science (IST — Technical University of Lisbon) and a PhD in Polymer Science and Engineering (University of Minho). Both my Master's Degree and PhD were on the modelling of polymer crystallization.

After my PhD, I invented and built, with other colleagues, a shear-DTA instrument and started a set of experiments to understand crystallization after shear deformations and the origin of the melt memory effect. Surprisingly, both amorphous and semicrystalline polymers respond similarly to shear deformations applied to the molten state. This result led me to study shear-induced structures and the effect of shear deformations and melt temperature on these structures. I've even estimated their size. However, I didn’t have access to experimental facilities or funding to perform the required studies.

Around 2012, I turned to molecular dynamics simulations without knowing anything about the subject. I received the assistance of Nuno Micaelo, who helped me set up the first systems and taught me the basics. A key requirement was that the systems should mimic, as closely as possible, the experimental behaviour of the materials. Later, I developed codes to read the trajectory files and analyse them on a Kuhn-segment basis. The first results were obtained through trial and error. Slowly (over about ten years) this work developed into a detailed analysis of melt morphology for entangled and unentangled systems at different temperatures, their crystallization, and their response to shear deformations.

So far, I’ve studied flexible and semiflexible polymer chains, amorphous polymers, and polymers with different tacticities. Almost all these results are still unpublished, mainly due to lack of time and to difficulties I’m facing at the institutional level.

For context on my position within the institution, see the section Institutional context below.

Research

What is an entanglement?

They are described as topological constraints or by “slip-links”. Both assume a binary interaction. In my view, topological constraints result from hard-core repulsive interactions and cannot explain the elastic properties demonstrated by entangled polymer chains. For a “slip-link” to exist, one has to consider a binary interaction between chain segments and associate to this interaction a potential energy. For this to occur, one has to look in detail at the morphology of the chain at the atomic scale.

Melt memory & crystallization

Kinetics under quiescent and flow. In start-up shear experiments, the viscosity of both semicrystalline and amorphous polymers saturates at steady state, corresponding to a loss of about one third of the initial entanglements. This loss, together with chain orientation, is responsible for the acceleration of crystallization kinetics in semicrystalline polymers.

Short-range order

This work — “Van der Waals Picture of Liquids, Solids, and Phase Transformations” by David Chandler, John D. Weeks, and Hans C. Andersen, (link) — illustrates the role of attractive and repulsive interactions on the system volume and on the local packing of molecules. I have identified the short-range order that explains the experimentally demonstrated “solid-like” behavior in polymer melts. In these melts, as in any other condensed phase, there are density fluctuations. In polymers, they occur when aligned chain segments interact with each other. I have demonstrated that the spatial arrangement of chain segments in these regions is the same as that in the crystalline unit cell of the polymer. It is remarkable that a fluid contains a “seed” with the information on the preferred spatial arrangement of the molecules in the solid state.

Software Repository

Fortran codes

(coming soon)

Executable and input data files to quantify heterogeneities (ACS, RCS and CE) in linear polymer chains.

Basics: The code reads LAMMPS .dump or .pdb coordinate files, wrapped or unwrapped. Files are placed in folders COORD or COORDPDB, respectively. Two input files are required: one describing the system (number of atoms, atoms per chain, chain types, atom types, atoms per repeat unit, pendant groups per repeat unit, and number of atoms per pendant group), and another specifying coordinate type, wrapping, and evaluation options (default characteristic ratio vs. frame-by-frame, RDF/structure factor/orientational correlations, local ACS analysis, short-range order identification, and aggregation rules based on relative orientation and separation distance).

Institutional context

I created this page to promote my scientific work after the page I had on my department’s site was closed and my name was removed from the department webpage. If you visit www.dep.uminho.pt, the site of the Department of Polymer Engineering, you will find a department without people, an “Internacionalização” (Internationalization) tab, but no English version of the site.

For many years, I have raised concerns, through other channels, about what I consider to be serious problems within my working institution, including issues of competence and academic integrity. In particular, I have expressed concerns about the appropriation of scientific work authored by others and published in high-impact journals (Nature, Science) being used in applications for funding from a state agency. A formal complaint has been submitted to the Attorney General's Office.

My position has affected my personal life and my career within the institution, and it is also one of the reasons for the difficulties I have faced in developing my work. To illustrate, I applied for Habilitation proofs. In addition to three pedagogical texts given freely to students (one in Polymer Physics, another in Thermodynamics of Materials, and one on Python recipes for treating experimental results on polymers, this one co-authored), each around 200 pages, I submitted a lesson, around 100 pages, with a compilation of some of my unpublished work, specifically devoted to addressing the physical nature of entanglements. My request was denied by six jury members — three from external institutions — three without knowledge of polymers, and three more focused on Polymer Processing, with some knowledge of general Polymer Science, but none with knowledge of Polymer Physics.
