About

I've graduated in Physics at the University of Lisbon. I started my scientific career in a State Laboratory, working with a Van de Graaff generator doing Rutherford backscattering experiments. I entered the polymer field after obtaining a degree in Materials Science (IST — Technical University of Lisbon) and a PhD in Polymer Science and Engineering (University of Minho). Both my Master's Degree and PhD were on the modelling of polymer crystallization.

After my PhD, I invented and built a shear-DTA instrument and started a set of experiments to understand crystallization after shear deformations and the origin of the melt memory effect. Surprisingly, both amorphous and semicrystalline polymers respond similarly to shear deformations applied to the molten state. This result led me to study shear-induced structures and the effect of shear deformations and melt temperature on these structures. I've even estimated their size. However, I didn’t have access to experimental facilities or funding to perform the required studies.

Around 2012, I turned to molecular dynamics simulations without knowing anything about the subject. I received the assistance of Nuno Micaelo, who helped me set up the first systems and taught me the basics. A key requirement was that the systems should mimic, as closely as possible, the experimental behaviour of the materials. Later, I developed codes to read the trajectory files and analyse them on a Kuhn-segment basis. The first results were obtained through trial and error. Slowly (over about ten years) this work developed into a detailed analysis of melt morphology for entangled and unentangled systems at different temperatures, their crystallization, and their response to shear deformations.

So far, I’ve studied flexible and semiflexible polymer chains, amorphous polymers, and polymers with different tacticities. Many of these results are still unpublished, mainly due to lack of time and to difficulties I’m facing at the institutional level.

If you prefer substance over volume, we’ll get along.

For context on my position within the institution, see the section Institutional context below.

Institutional context

I created this page to promote my scientific work after the page I had on my department’s site was closed and my name was removed from the department webpage. If you visit www.dep.uminho.pt, the site of the Department of Polymer Engineering, you will find a department without people.

In recent years I have raised concerns, through other channels, about what I consider to be serious problems within my working institution, including issues of competence and academic integrity. In particular, I have expressed concerns about the appropriation of scientific work authored by others and published in high-impact journals (Nature, Science) being used in applications for funding from a state agency. A formal complaint has been submitted to the Attorney General's Office.

This situation has affected my personal life and my career within the institution, and it is also one of the reasons for the difficulties I have faced in developing my work. To illustrate, I applied for Habilitation proofs. In addition to three pedagogical texts (one in Polymer Physics, another in Thermodynamics of Materials, and one on Python recipes for treating experimental results on polymers), each around 200 pages, I submitted a lesson, also around 200 pages, devoted to the physical nature of entanglements and the molecular origin of the melt-memory effect. My request was denied by six jury members — three from external institutions — four without any knowledge of polymers, one with some knowledge of polymer science, but none with knowledge of Polymer Physics.

These notes are included here to give context to my publication record and current circumstances.
